Case Study

UVA・Design System

The design system behind a complex premium hospitality product.

Role Product Designer
Year 2025
Scope UX/UI Design, Design System
Tools Desk Research, Academic Articles, Book References, Miro, Dovetail, FigJam, Figma

Overview

A product with more than one problem to solve

UVA started as a product for premium restaurants focused on financial management. But that quickly proved to be an insufficient definition. As research progressed, it became clearer that finances were just the visible center of a much denser operation. Costs, stock, suppliers, staff, reservations, and customer experience were all interconnected.

The product didn't just need good flows. It needed structure to handle that interdependence.

Discovery

The discovery work

The project started broad on purpose, to avoid an oversimplified reading of the problem. The mind map and stakeholder map helped reveal that the operation didn't revolve solely around the manager, but around a network of relationships and decisions that affected the business on multiple levels.

When research deepened into financial and operational routines, the tension became evident: the restaurant was running, but it still relied too heavily on human effort, fragmented tools, and manual workarounds to maintain quality and predictability.

Scope

The point where the interface could collapse

This complexity showed its force when the scope began to consolidate. The product was already pointing toward real-time financial reports, dish profitability, smarter reservations, customer history, supplier management, automatic ingredient control, and analytics dashboards. All of that made sense.

The problem was different: UVA was beginning to bring together very different contexts within the same system — analytical, operational, transactional, and relational. And continuing to solve this flow by flow would have been a short-sighted decision.

"That's when the design system landed in my hands."

Ownership

Taking responsibility for the system

From that point on, my work shifted from just designing interfaces to structuring the foundation that would allow the product to grow with coherence. I didn't treat the design system as a library for organizing files. I treated it as infrastructure. Something that needed to support real evolution, reduce ambiguity, and prevent the product from losing readability as it gained new modules, states, and interaction patterns.

I structured the system in clear layers — onboarding, principles, foundations, base components, complex components, and contextual application — because I wanted it to be intelligible, not just complete. That kind of architecture makes a difference when the system starts to grow. Instead of accumulating pieces, it becomes readable. Anyone who enters can quickly understand what's foundational, what's a recurring pattern, and where complexity truly lives.

Scale

Building for scale, not for screenshots

One of the most important decisions was building the DS with variables from the start. That gave the system a real backbone. Instead of treating color, typography, numbers, and behavior as scattered choices across screens, I organized a logic of primitives, semantics, and component-oriented tokens. The immediate benefit was consistency. The more important benefit was predictability.

In a product with dashboards, dense inputs, navigation, operational states, and composite components, that predictability was what made the system scalable.

Support for light and dark mode was born within that same logic. It wasn't a later adaptation. It was designed as part of the architecture. That brought more rigor to naming, inheriting, and applying tokens, but it allowed us to arrive at a system with real parity between themes. What mattered there wasn't offering two versions of the interface. It was ensuring that the same structure could sustain contrast, hierarchy, and identity in any usage context.

Documentation

The documentation that made the system usable

The part I value most in this work is the documentation. I didn't want a design system that only worked well for the person who created it. I wanted a system that could be understood, used, and expanded without relying on constant verbal explanation. That's why the documentation was designed as part of the product.

For simpler components, that meant documenting structure, sizes, states, and application. For more complex ones, I went beyond visual anatomy and brought in behavior, composition, implementation logic, and usage guidance.

The inputs illustrate this well. Instead of documenting a collection of loose fields, I designed a family of components with a shared base and consistent specializations. Select Single, for example, was structured to adapt to contexts like avatar, flag, unit, quantity, and date picker without losing coherence. Input TAG, Inputs & Selects, and Input Text Area follow the same logic. This allowed the system to grow through specialization, not duplication.

The same reasoning appears in more composite components, like Accordion. The documentation didn't stop at the finished piece. It included context, usage scenarios, composition, and comparative guidance on when that pattern made more sense than other alternatives. That detail significantly changes the role of the design system. The component stops being just reusable and starts carrying enough context to support decision-making.

"Owning the design system meant owning the responsibility for what would sustain everything else."

Reflection

Why this work matters

In the end, the value of the UVA Design System wasn't in looking well-organized. It was in being able to sustain a product that was born with real complexity. Well-structured variables, themes built into the architecture, robust documentation, and a componentization logic that allowed flexibility without turning into chaos.

That's why this project remains important to me. It started with a fairly clear business problem — control, predictability, efficiency — but it was when the solution began to scale that my work took on a different weight.

Owning the design system meant owning the responsibility for what would sustain everything else. And, to me, that says much more about how I think about product than any final mockup.

Final flow

From system to experience

I closed the project by applying the design system to a real flow, making it easier to show how it supports product decisions at scale — not just interface consistency.
